### Preface

&emsp;&emsp; Mastery on the basics of grammar is one of the tools in enhancing one's communicative skills. It is a guide to an individual in constructing correct sentences and paragraphs both in written and oral aspects. Almost all activities in school, at work, at home or anywhere includes relaying of information, expressing ideas and feelings, transmitting all kinds of messages and writing correspondence. All of these require correctness in grammar to fully understand the meaning of messages we receive; if these messages are not writen using correct grammar, then miscommunication will take place and will eventually lead to wrong information or feedback. Thus, it is necessary to master the basics of grammar to facilitate communicative types of activities.

&emsp;&emsp; This book gives you a comprehensive discussion of the rules of grammar and exercises that will test your mastery on the rules.

### Lesson 1 - Spelling

&emsp;&emsp; Effective written communication calls not only for correct word order and appropriate diction, but also uniform spelling. Mastery of a language includes both the ability to speak it and write it accordingly to the forms accepted by the educated and refined people of the community.

&emsp;&emsp; Spelling is the written representation of the sounds of spoken words. In English, there is a definite but disconcerting relationship between sound and spelling and this has resulted in many spelling problems.

&emsp;&emsp; Much of the difficulty in spelling is caused by the fact that the English language has developed only twenty-six letters (the graphemes of English) to represent more than forty different sounds (or phonemes). Thus, the same letter may represent more than one sound as does the letter *a* in *ape*, *car*, and *fat* and the letter *c* in *cat*, and *cent*. Note the variety of sounds represent by the same letters in *cough*, *bough*, *through*, and *plough*. And the same sound is not always represented by the same letter or letters as in such as *blue* and *blew*, *peace* and *piece*, *pray*, *prey* and *weight*. Furthermore, the most common phoneme or sound, the schwa [e] may be represented by any of the vowel letters or almost any possible combinations of them. In English, a vowel, a dipththong, or a consonant letter, therefore, may represent two or more sounds and various letters may represent the same sounds.

&emsp;&emsp; Another complication arises from words with silent or unsounded letters: *pneumonia*, *Wednesday*, *palm*, *listen*, *gnaw*, *knife*, *debt*.

&emsp;&emsp; Another cause of the spelling problem isthe fact that while pronunciation has undergone changes, the alphabet has not. For example, the final *e* in *ride* and in *bake* was sounded, and the word *knight* was pronounced as spelled during the Middle English period. But the spellings of these words have remained although their pronunciations have changed.

&emsp;&emsp; A number of spelling problems in English have come from fact that the English language is a hybrid of many languages. The inconsistent nature of English spelling, however, does not justify your misspellings. It is the task of every student of English who desires to communicate effectively to learn to spell correctly.
